Focus To-Do

    Rethinking how to increase users willingness to manage their time with FOCUS TO-DO.

BACKGROUND

Focus To-Do is a time management app that is designed around the Pomodoro Technique - one of the most popular productivity strategies. As one of the users, I define this is an app with useful functions but less intuitive. I started this project with many assumptions around things that matter for humans to manage their time with an app. To challenge these assumptions and discover new insights, I analyzed 4 other time management apps and looked at the mindset of users. I interviewed 5 frequent users of the time management apps, and 80% of them have used Focus To-Do previously. These interviews helped me to identify 3 key problems to address.

Side note: This is a self-directed project. I am not affiliated with Focus To-Do.


THE PAIN POINT

80% of users indicated that they had a certain degree of difficulty when they started to this app.

PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS

PROBLEM 1

Unfriendly experience when creating and starting a task timer.

Lack of visual content and unfriendly flow design create obstacle to users. The main page was simply a list without any main functions, which differs from most users' expectations. The main two functions - create a task and start a timer, are in other seperate pages. The unintuitive design hurts users experience, which in turn leads users to switch to other time management app.

Solution: Reimagine users need for a time management system.

Inefficient ways of presentations make the majority of the information displayed on the main page irrelevant to user needs. To provide a better user experience design, I moved the functions of creating a task and starting the timer to the main page, which helps users to use the app more intuitively.

PROBLEM 2

Feel stressed to manage the time.

Lack of consistency and motivation are two of the most common problems that stop people from pursuing a new habit. As a human being, it is natural for us to revert to familiar structures. Building a new habit usually often takes extra effort, and people tend to avoid effort. Thus, helping users to build up their time management habit is also a key to increase the usage and user retention rate. (As this is a personal project, I have no quantitative data to back up this hypothesis, and only qualitative data collected from user interviews.)

Solution: Help users to conquer time management.

There is a 3R principle (reminder, routine, and reward) for building and maintaining habits. The app already had the first two R's, so my solution focused on “reward" to attract and motivate users to keep managing their time with this app. I created a new coupon feature, letting users earn real-world discounts by accumulating their focusing time.

PROBLEM 3

The theme color and style limit the recutment the potential users.

During the interview, I had noticed that theme color and the app style are also concedered when deciding whether to use the app. Furthermore, the bright pink theme color for this app had reduced some users willing to use it.

Solution: Redesign the style for more users.

Redesigning the theme color and the whole style that is acceptable by more people.

DESIGN PROCESS

01. - Competitive Analysis

I first looked into the market to examine whether existing time management applications can help people better manage their time. I also observed whether they have any special solutions to attract users.

(From left to right) Focus To-Do, Be Focus, FocusKeeper, Focus Time-Management, and Pomodoro with White Noice.

What fun facts have I found?

  • Besides Focus To-Do, other apps all use a countdown timer as their main page.
  • Focus To-Do, Be Focus, and Focus apps have a laptop version for people to use on their computers.
  • FocusKeeper has a timer and an analysis function but does not have a to-do list.
  • None of these apps have special functions.

02. - User Interviews

After understanding the business side of other competitors, I started to focus on users. I tried to understand more about the challenges that arose while people use this kind of mobile application. I interviewed 5 frequent users of the time management apps. I used the concept of Simon Sinek's golden circle to understand the why, how, and what of interviewees' time-management needs.

What problems have I noticed?

  • Most of the interviewees want to have a better use for their time and to understand accurately the time they spend on each category or task.
  • Interviewees all difficulty using time-management apps for a long time, and soon stop using the app.
  • Some interviewees are lazy to set up the task list.
  • To use this kind of app, interviewees need to be used to many new things such as type in the task they are going to do, start the timer before doing a task, and stop the timer after they finish it.
  • Theme color influences user's decision on whether to use the application. The current theme color for Focus To-Do is pink, which stops many male interviewees from using it.

03. - Persona & User Story

To better understand the target users, I synthesized the interview results and came up with the persona.
Based on the Natalie's goal and her needs, I also came up with user stories:
  1. Natalie wants to manage her time better so she can achieve her goals.
  2. Natalie wants to finish her assignments on time so she can get a better grade.
  3. Natalie wants to know the accurate amount of time that she spends on each task so she can learn what type of work should she put more effort into.

04. - Journey map

Before starting with the design and layout of our features’ wireframes, user journey is also an important part that needs to be understood. Through this step I can learn more detailed feelings of users from each step.

What opportunities have I found?

  • The list is the main page should be replaced to the countdown timer.
  • Information is not well organized. In the main page, the day of tasks and the category of tasks are put on the same list. This makes users confused.
  • The app is not attractive enough for people to use it for a long time.

05. - Sketches &Wireframe

With my research findings and personas in mind, I started to brainstorm and design a solution that can solve current problems and increase people's interest.

What main features have I chosen?

  • Redefined the countdown timer on the main page.
  • Recategorized the information and reorganized the list page.
  • Created a new coupon feature, so people can use the focusing time that they accumulated to exchange to real world discounts.

06. - Usability test

I followed Steve Krug’s template and KJ analysis as my usability testing. I am impressed by the feedback I got from the participants. It is fun to gain different perspectives from different people, especially when they are using the app and are trying to share their concerns for each step. Based on the result, I can learn what is their favorite part and what confuses them.

Detail Test Rrport (.pdf)

What designs have I changed?

  • Added a navigation bar and made the "add task" function appear on more pages.
  • Showed the progress to let users know the total estimated time they would spend on the task.
  • Removed the guide page for very-first-time users, since the design should be based on users' mental model without learning anything before they use this app.

FINAL DESIGN

Timer

Natalie now can see the countdown timer and choose the task that she wants to do directly after she opens the app. This page also provides the total estimated time for the task, so she knows how much time she will need to spend on this task.

Daily To-Do List

This list shows Natalie her daily tasks organized by category and listed from high priority to low priority. Natalie can choose to see other dates' to-do lists by changing the date on the top of the list page. Also, she can add a new task in one step by inputting simple information.

Coupon

With this new function, Natalie now uses this app to help her manage time. Although building a new habit is challenging, the coupon function also helps her to buy things for a cheaper price and saves money. This keep Natalie using it.

REFLECTION

What I have learned from this project...
  • Reframing problems
    In the very beginning, I thought this project is only an interface problem. I can solve the problem by providing a better interface design and user flow. However, after I talked with interviewees, I have found out that the core problem of current situation is more about human factors. Thus, I need to redefine the problem and find the real prolem that is behind the screen.
  • Giving up a normal solution for a better one
    To give up a solution that I already have, and to seek a better one is always hard. But since I reframed the problem, I need to throw away the old idea for a new to come. It is hard to do so, but the feedback from users let me know that this is the right decision.
  • Designers should not only focus on design
    Though I know this theory, it is hard to fulfill in reality. In my previous prototype version, the add button was on the main page, but since I don’t like that interface design very much, I removed the add button. However, after the usability test, I have realized how a big mistake I made.
  • Don't make users think
    I used to have a guide page in my initial version for the first time users. However, after the first usability test, I found out that users easily forget where to find the specific function. A good design should follow users' mental model and let them easily and quickly find the function they want. And also, although my app is based on a pomodoro technique, I should not think that everyone using my application knows it. I should apply the technique in the app without letting users' learn, and let users' gain the benefit from the technique directly.
